## On Building Without Customers

> "Every day I say, maybe I need to add a feature or make the mobile app, but I know this is not the right thing to do; I need to find people who want it."
- Hacker News, "I built a SaaS that nobody asked for, now what?"

> "After 4 months of coding nights and weekends, I launched it on Product Hunt, Indie Hackers, Twitter, and Reddit. I expected a modest response. A few upvotes, maybe a couple of signups. Instead, I got crickets."
- Dev.to, "10 Key Lessons from My Failed SaaS Launch"

> "I had poured months into something that nobody wanted. I felt like a failure. Like maybe I wasn't cut out for SaaS."
- Dev.to

> "I thought I was solving a pain. But what I really built was a nice-to-have tool."
- Dev.to

> "What I didn't expect was how many other challenges came with launching a SaaS business. Marketing, legal matters, and handling taxes quickly became part of my daily routine — areas I had barely touched as a freelancer."
- Medium, "Vibe coding as a Solo Developer"

## On Marketing Struggles

> "Since developers are not used to selling their products, when they have no users they assume it's because they have still not built the right feature. So they end up building tons of features that no one wants instead of going out there looking for customers."
- Indie Hackers

> "The problem common for developers is they love building. But building isn't enough for a successful business - it's 90% marketing and selling."
- Indie Hackers

> "Marketing often feels like storytelling mixed with psychology — domains that weren't covered in computer science curriculum."
- Medium, "Why Technical Founders Struggle with Marketing"

> "Customers don't buy features; they buy outcomes. They don't care that you're using microservices architecture; they care that your app loads 3 seconds faster than the competition."
- Medium

> "Software engineers might stick with what's comfortable, trying to suppress problems in areas like marketing and finance. Instead of focusing on expanding their network, they keep infinitely improving the software."
- Indie Hackers

## On Sales Fear

> "Cold calling is frightening if you're an introverted engineer."
- Hacker News

> "I knew marketing would be important, and sales would be important when I started this thing... but I really didn't realize that when I quit, what I actually was doing was making a job change from 'really pretty good engineer to full time sales guy.'"
- Hacker News

> "I had to learn how to prospect, cold call, drill into people's pain points, and find decision makers. It's not easy."
- Hacker News

> "As a software developer, it's really hard for me to get started on just talking to potential customers."
- A Smart Bear blog

## On Vibe Coding Problems

> "My saas was built with Cursor, zero hand written code... Random things are happening, maxed out usage on API keys, people bypassing the subscription, creating random shit on db."
- Documented case study

> "Cursor keeps breaking other parts of the code."
- Documented case study

> "If you close your eyes and you don't look at the code and you have AIs build things with shaky foundations as you add another floor, and another floor, and another floor, and another floor, things start to kind of crumble."
- Michael Truell, Cursor CEO

> "Vibe coding might work for quickly mocking up a game or website, but more advanced projects face real risks."
- Fortune

> "The invisible complexity gap: the difference between 'it works on my machine' and 'it's secure in production.'"
- Industry analysis

> "You can't secure what you don't understand, and you don't understand what AI builds for you."
- Security expert analysis

## On The Reality Check

> "They don't show you the months they made $0 while working 12hr days."
- The Senior Dev blog

> "Most 'solo developers' end up burnt out, broke and going back to their corporate job in a few months. That's because they underestimate the effort. And they overestimate how easy it will be."
- The Senior Dev blog

> "The hard part is getting people to pay for your 'vibe-coded' app."
- The Senior Dev blog

> "You have to do everything (including sh**t you don't like) – coding, support, marketing, accounting, video shooting and editing – all while you get paid less."
- The Senior Dev blog

> "Maybe people didn't understand the vision. Maybe better marketing was needed. But the truth was simpler: the founder was solving a problem that didn't exist."

## On Burnout

> "Most people think that developer burnout comes from coding too much. But developers rarely burn out from writing code—they burn out from everything that prevents them from doing it well."
- The Senior Dev blog

> "Most developers don't burn out from writing code—they burn out from unclear priorities, constant interruptions, and politics."
- The Senior Dev blog

> "Burnout is extremely common among software developers. Some statistics claim that up to 80% of programmers feel burned out."
- Industry statistics

## On First Customers

> "It's one of the hardest steps in a startup, getting that first rube to part with their money over your barely-minimum barely-viable product."
- A Smart Bear blog

> "The fallacy here is that you can copy what I did and get a customer. The truth is that even I cannot copy what I did to get my first customer."
- A Smart Bear blog

> "Instead of finding potential customers (which is hard), developers often just make a product (which is easy for them). But that's how you build a hobby, not a company."
- A Smart Bear blog

## On What Works

> "If you want to go from 0 to $10K MRR you should divide your time 50:50 between coding and marketing."
- Jon Yongfook

> "I should've aggressively marketed the product, built it in public, made people curious about it and only spent a tiny amount of time actually building it."
- Indie hacker who launched 37 products

> "Charging early is not greedy. Charging early is validation. If someone is willing to pay for your product when it is still new and imperfect, it means you have real value in your hands."
- SaaS guide

> "You aren't selling based on an MVP that you've built, but based on the problem they have and what the solution may look like. A sketch of the UI is often enough if the problem you're solving is real."
- Indie Hackers

> "Just because you don't have users on day 1 doesn't mean you should give up."
- Indie Hackers
